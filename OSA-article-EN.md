# OSA z.s.: Who Pays, Who Inherits, and Who Gets Paid

### An explainer on collective copyright management in the Czech Republic — its money, its critics, and its law

**Author's disclosure.** The author of this text has been a registered author (member rightsholder) of OSA – Ochranný svaz autorský pro práva k dílům hudebním, z.s. since 29 April 2022, and works professionally as a music producer and front-of-house sound engineer. He therefore stands on both sides of the arrangement described below: he pays collective-management fees when he works with recorded music, and he is paid by OSA as an author. This text aims for factual precision and balance rather than advocacy. Every figure and legal claim is sourced in the Notes at the end; readers are invited to check them.

**Status.** This is a public working draft, published for comment before any wider release. Corrections and counter-arguments are welcome.

---

## 1. "OSA Nostra": a distrust older than Czech capitalism

In September 1989 — before the Velvet Revolution — the songwriter and journalist Michal Horáček, together with his editorial colleague Luboš Beniak, published an exposé in the widely read magazine *Mladý svět* under the title **"OSA Nostra."** It alleged mafia-like practices inside the communist-era collecting society. The pun stuck. More than three decades later, "OSA Nostra" is still the epithet critics reach for. [1]

That longevity is the first thing worth understanding. Public suspicion of OSA is not a product of modern licensing disputes over café radios; it predates the market economy itself. Any honest account has to separate that inherited reflex from the specific, verifiable facts of how the organisation works today.

## 2. What collective management actually is

OSA is a **collective manager** (*kolektivní správce*) operating under the Czech Copyright Act, **Act No. 121/2000 Coll.** Its function is narrow and specific: it licenses the use of musical works on behalf of the people who hold the rights to them — composers, lyricists, publishers, and their heirs — collects the money that use generates, deducts its costs, and distributes the rest. [2]

An individual songwriter cannot realistically license and invoice every radio station, streaming service, restaurant, and concert promoter that plays their work, then verify each use and chase each payment. Collective management exists to solve that coordination problem at scale. In exchange for that service, OSA holds a **statutory territorial position** as the sole collective manager for musical authors' rights in the Czech Republic. There is no competing society an author or a business can choose instead. This monopoly is the structural root of most of the conflict described in this text: the people who pay cannot vote with their feet, and the people who are paid cannot either.

Three provisions of the Copyright Act recur throughout the disputes below, and are worth stating plainly:

- **§ 18(3)** — merely *operating a device capable of transmission* (a television or radio set standing on business premises) does **not**, by itself, constitute "communication to the public" (*sdělování veřejnosti*). [3]
- **§ 30** — use that does **not** serve to achieve "direct or indirect economic benefit" is not treated as public use. [4]
- **§ 23** — the provision governing "operation of radio and television broadcasting," rewritten by the 2023 amendment discussed in Section 4.

Since 2018, OSA has also acted as the **single collection point** (*jednotné inkasní místo*) for all Czech collective managers. A business now signs one licence contract with OSA covering both live and recorded music, and OSA forwards the money to the other societies — INTERGRAM (performers and producers), DILIA, OOA-S and OAZA — according to their tariffs. [5] This is why the long-standing complaint about "paying OSA and Intergram separately" is now, administratively, a single payment — though it still funds two distinct streams of rights: **authors** on the one hand, **performers and producers** on the other.

One further legal fact underpins the entire question of inheritance: economic copyright (*autorská majetková práva*) **cannot be transferred between living persons** (*inter vivos*), but it **passes to heirs on death** and endures, in simplified terms, for **70 years after the author's death**. [6] That is why "successors in interest" exist as a category at all, and why OSA pays royalties to families decades after a composer has died.

## 3. The money: OSA in numbers (2024)

The figures below are drawn from OSA's 2024 annual report. They matter because most of the public debate is conducted without them. [7]

| Total collected for all Czech collective managers | **1.87 billion CZK** (+9.02% year on year) |
| Collected for OSA and foreign rightsholders | **1.58 billion CZK** (+9.64%) |
| Distributed to music authors and publishers | **1.18 billion CZK** (823m domestic, 356m foreign) |
| Average overhead (cost-to-collection) ratio | **~12%** — i.e. an author receives about **88 CZK of every 100** collected |
| Domestic rightsholders under contract | **11,977** — of which 8,791 authors, **3,012 heirs**, 174 publishers |
| Voting members | **611** (including 50 heirs and 31 publishers) |

| Average annual royalty per rightsholder paid | **5,547 CZK** |

| Business partners (by company ID) | **34,176** |
| Payout frequency | **Monthly** (since 2021) |

Two of these numbers deserve to be read together, because between them they frame the internal debate covered in Section 6.

First, the **average annual royalty of 5,547 CZK**. An average is not a typical case; it is inflated by a small number of very large earners. The great majority of registered authors receive far less. Collective management, in other words, distributes a large sum of money to a population in which most members earn very little and a few earn a great deal — a shape the system inherits from the music market itself, not one OSA invents.

Second, the governance ratio: **8,791 authors under contract, but only 611 with a vote.** The people who formally control OSA — who elect its bodies and approve its rules — are a small fraction of the people on whose behalf it collects. Whether that concentration is a reasonable membership threshold or a democratic deficit is a fair question, and one this text raises rather than settles.

## 4. The first side: business owners who pay

This is the loudest constituency, and the one whose grievance is most often voiced in public. Its core is simple: **you pay for the right to have music audible on your premises, whether or not music is the point of your business.**

Some concrete, documented cases give the complaint a human shape.

A hairdresser, **Hana Hamouzová in Libčice**, originally paid OSA 1,500 CZK a year plus a Czech Radio concession fee of 540 CZK. She was then additionally required by INTERGRAM to pay a further 1,900 CZK a year for the same radio playing in her salon — and, she said, had to raise her prices by more than ten per cent to absorb it. [8]

The tariffs themselves are modest per unit but cumulative. In 2016, a pub or shop in a municipality of fewer than 1,000 inhabitants, playing nothing more than an ordinary radio, paid OSA **148.80 CZK a month** and INTERGRAM **133 CZK** — roughly **282 CZK a month** combined, scaling upward with the size of the municipality and the number and type of devices. [9] The rates then rose sharply: OSA announced increases of **up to 50%** from 2017, prompting mayors from the Association of Local Governments (*Sdružení místních samospráv*) to file a complaint with the Czech competition authority (ÚOHS) alleging abuse of a dominant position. OSA defended the rise by pointing to substantially higher rates in countries such as Hungary and Croatia. [10] A further increase of **10.7%** followed for 2024. [11]

Some businesses have responded not by litigating but by leaving the repertoire entirely: switching to web-based or in-store radio services that play only music by authors and performers **not** represented by OSA or INTERGRAM, and therefore carrying no fee. **Kavárna Továrna in Zlín** (co-owner Tomáš Novotný) is one documented example. [11] This is a legal and quietly growing form of protest — and, incidentally, a market signal that the monopoly is not absolute at the level of repertoire.

Enforcement is real and systematic. The collecting societies carry out on the order of **hundreds of on-site inspections a month.** A business found operating without a licence is given a deadline to conclude one; if it refuses, the societies claim **unjust enrichment** (*bezdůvodné obohacení*), which the law sets at **double** the licence fee that should have been paid, plus court costs if the business loses. [12] The volume of resulting litigation is considerable: the Municipal Court in Prague alone registered **more than 4,000** such cases from 2010 onward — OSA responsible for 1,830 of them, INTERGRAM 1,413, and the collective manager OAZA 846. [13]

### The 2023 "lex železářství": relief that mostly wasn't

In January 2023 an amendment to § 23 of the Copyright Act took effect, popularly nicknamed **"lex železářství"** (the "hardware-shop law") and presented politically as freeing small shops, hairdressers and similar trades from fees for a radio playing in the background. In practice, lawyers say it changed very little. The attorney **Marek Martinka** of the firm Maaars put it bluntly: *"Lex železářství v podstatě nepřináší nic nového. Změny se ve výsledku netýkají nikoho"* — "Lex železářství essentially brings nothing new. In the end the changes affect no one." [14]

The reason is that exemption requires **four conditions to be met simultaneously**: the playback must be **incidental** (*nahodilá produkce*), reach only a **narrow circle of persons** (*úzký okruh osob*), be **independent of the listeners' wishes**, and be of a **non-commercial character**. Meeting two or three is not enough. [15] OSA's own reading, published on its website, is that the amendment "merely codified conditions already established by existing court decisions," and that a radio audible in the customer-facing area of a shop or restaurant still triggers the licensing obligation. For music played only in employee areas not accessible to customers, OSA still requires a licence, though at a rate 50% below the standard shop tariff. [16] OSA's chairman, **Roman Strejček**, estimates the exemption applies in at most one case in a hundred, and rejects the common defence head-on: *"Oblíbený argument, že do holičství nebo obchodu si zákazníci nechodí prvoplánově poslechnout hudbu, neobstojí"* — "The popular argument that customers don't come to a barber or a shop primarily to listen to music does not hold." [17] INTERGRAM, for its part, has warned that a business which stops paying without genuinely qualifying can be pursued for back-fees at double the usual rate. [18]

Not every lawyer agrees with OSA's narrow reading. Others — including Jiří Matzner and the attorney Petr Molnár — have argued that existing case law, now codified, already exempts many small establishments (bike shops, hairdressers, coffee roasteries, hardware stores, locksmiths, craftsmen's workshops), where only a limited number of people, perhaps up to twenty, are present at once and the broadcast is incidental and non-commercial. [19] The disagreement is genuine and unresolved, which is precisely why so many of these cases end up in court.

### Where businesses have won

The picture is not one-sided. Czech and European courts have repeatedly checked the collecting societies, and the resulting case law is the businesses' strongest ammunition.

- In **III. ÚS 1598/19 (14 January 2020)**, the Constitutional Court sided with a clothing retailer, CHARLEY FASHION, against OSA, and placed the **burden of proof on the collective manager**: to win, OSA must specify — at least by genre — that protected works were *actually performed* through the device. A court's finding that music was merely "clearly audible" on an inspection recording is legally insufficient. [20] The ruling built on an earlier one to the same effect, III. ÚS 3102/16 of 21 May 2019. [21]
- In **III. ÚS 2429/14 (2015)**, the Constitutional Court quashed a judgment that had ordered a shop operator (STAVES s.r.o.) to pay INTERGRAM 2,721.80 CZK, holding that courts "must not proceed formalistically" in disputes with collective managers and must examine whether a public performance actually took place. [22]
- In 2014 the Constitutional Court overturned a ruling against the bike shop **Velo CZ** in Hradec Králové, ordering a fresh assessment of whether the listeners even constituted a "public" under EU case law; and a restaurateur, **René Vaněček**, won his case by playing only music from regional bands who had given him permission directly. [23]
- The Court of Justice of the European Union has likewise held that a **dentist** playing music in his surgery owes no fee, because his patients are not a "public" in the relevant sense. [24]

Honesty requires the counterweight: **the collecting societies still win most disputes with businesses.** [23] The victories above are the exceptions that mark the outer boundary of what "public performance" means — but they are exceptions, not the rule.

### The spa cases and the "slippery slope"

The most consequential business dispute reached the European Court of Justice. In 2011 OSA sued around fifteen Czech spa houses for unpaid fees for televisions and radios in guest rooms, with members of the Union of Medicinal Spas collectively sued for close to 10 million CZK. [25] One of those actions — OSA's claim of **546,995 CZK against Léčebné lázně Mariánské Lázně** — became **Case C-351/12**, decided by the CJEU on **27 February 2014**. The Court held that deliberately piping broadcasts into patients' rooms *is* a "communication to the public" under Article 3(1) of Directive 2001/29, and that the Czech statutory exemption for healthcare establishments was therefore incompatible with EU law. [26]

But the same judgment cut two ways. The Court also held that OSA's territorial monopoly does **not, in itself,** violate EU law, because territory-based collective management serves legitimate copyright-protection interests — *and* it warned that fees "appreciably higher than those charged in other Member States," without objective justification, would themselves indicate an **abuse of a dominant position** under Article 102 TFEU. [26] That single sentence handed businesses a benchmark they still use to challenge tariff levels. Reporting at the time noted the wider stakes: if OSA prevailed against the spas, "the next target would undoubtedly be hospitals." [25]

## 5. The second side: heirs and successors in interest

This is the least-documented perspective, and the one that produced the most surprising material.

When an author dies, their economic rights pass through **probate** (*dědické řízení*). An heir becomes a recognised holder of the rights (*nositel autorských práv*) only once a court has confirmed the inheritance; they cannot simply present themselves to a collecting society and claim royalties. [27] Where several heirs inherit the rights to a single work, they do not hold divisible shares *in the work itself* but only shares in the **common revenue** it generates — a distinction that shapes how OSA pays out to families. [28] OSA maintains a dedicated intake for this: an online registration form covering three categories (heirs under foreign law, heirs of an author who never had an OSA contract, and heirs of an author whose OSA contract predates 2002), and a heirs' desk reachable at *dedici@osa.cz* for anyone whose situation does not fit the form. [29] At the end of 2024 OSA represented **3,012 heirs**, of whom only 50 held voting membership. [7]

The scale of what is at stake is illustrated by the best-known case. The heirs of **Karel Gott** continue to receive substantial royalties administered by OSA and INTERGRAM; published estimates of the annual sum vary, with reporting placing it somewhere between roughly **three and eight million crowns a year**. [30] A nuance worth explaining to readers: because roughly 98% of Gott's songs were written by others, much of that income flows through **INTERGRAM as a performer**, not through OSA as an author — a clean illustration of the author/performer split that confuses so much of the public debate. [31]

The genuinely troubling material concerns not OSA but the **probate system feeding it.** There is a documented failure mode in which notaries, unfamiliar with what music rights are, classify inherited copyright or performers' rights as *nepatrný majetek* — property of negligible value — and hand them to whoever arranged the funeral. One reported case involved the rights of a significant performing artist, with expected future collective-management income running into the hundreds of thousands of crowns, released to the funeral arranger as if they were worthless. [32] Heirs are also sometimes refused registration or payout by collecting societies when their succession is not properly documented in the probate file. [32] For an heir, in other words, the greatest risk is often not that OSA will withhold money, but that the value of the inheritance will be lost or misdirected before it ever reaches OSA at all — through a notary's misunderstanding of an asset that does not look like an asset.

## 6. The third side: the authors OSA collects for

For the members themselves — the constituency this author belongs to — the assessment is genuinely mixed, and the fair thing is to give both halves.

**The case in favour.** Registration with OSA is currently free of charge, done through an online form, after which the author gains access to OSA's INFOSA information system. [33] OSA distributes royalties **monthly**, which is faster than many foreign societies that pay only annually. [34] Its overhead of around 12% is in line with comparable European societies, and its tariffs are public and approved annually by the Ministry of Culture. [35][7] Above all, OSA performs a task no individual author could: tracking millions of micro-uses across broadcast, streaming and live performance, in the Czech Republic and — through reciprocal agreements with foreign societies covering some 5.2 million rightsholders — abroad. [7] For a working author, this is not a trivial convenience; it is the difference between being paid for foreign airplay and never knowing it happened.

**The case against.** The two numbers from Section 3 return here. An average annual royalty of **5,547 CZK**, set against a membership in which only **611 of 8,791 authors** hold a vote, is the empirical basis for the recurring complaint that the system serves large earners well and small ones thinly, and that control is concentrated among those who benefit most. This is not an accusation of wrongdoing; it is a structural observation, and a reasonable subject for reform debate.

### Case study: the OSA Prizes and Hard Rico

A vivid, recent illustration of how OSA operates by metric rather than judgment came in June 2026. At the **21st OSA Prizes**, held on **10 June 2026** at the ARCHA+ theatre in Prague, the society named the rapper **Hard Rico** (Enrico Pešta) the most successful composer and lyricist of popular music for the year 2025. Just two months earlier, the Regional Court in Ostrava had sentenced him — as the organiser of a brutal robbery and extortion in Ostrava-Poruba — to four years' imprisonment and a two-million-crown fine; by the time of the ceremony the conviction was final (*pravomocně odsouzen*). [36][37]

The juxtaposition revived the old "OSA Nostra" epithet in the press. But it also revealed something about the institution's design rather than its morals. The OSA Prizes are awarded, in OSA's own words, "on the basis of exact statistics of airplay, streaming and live performance of works, not on any moral evaluation of the authors" — no expert jury decides; the winners simply reflect what the public actually played, streamed and bought. [38] By that measure Hard Rico genuinely was the most-played popular author of the year, and OSA's numbers reported it.

Whether a purely statistical honour is the right model for a body that also presents itself as a custodian of national musical culture is a fair question — and the Hard Rico case is the sharpest possible way to ask it. But it is worth seeing clearly what the alternative would require. The mechanism that makes OSA fair to a small, unconnected author — *you are paid, and counted, for exactly what the public plays, and for nothing else* — is the very same mechanism that will crown a convicted criminal when the public streams him most. The society cannot easily keep the first virtue while discarding the second; they are one and the same rule. That is the honest tension at the heart of a metric-driven collecting society, and it is not resolved by outrage.

## 7. The regulatory and judicial record

The hardest evidence about OSA's conduct comes not from opinion but from the competition authority and the courts.

**The antitrust fines.** In a decision of **18 December 2019**, the Czech competition authority (ÚOHS) fined OSA **10,676,000 CZK** for abuse of a dominant position; the appeal (*rozklad*) was dismissed on **27 November 2020**. [39] A parallel decision fined **INTERGRAM 20,799,000 CZK**, confirmed on appeal by the ÚOHS chairman Petr Mlsna on **4 June 2021.** [40] The conduct in both cases was the same: between 2008 and 2014 (for INTERGRAM, 1 January 2009 to 6 November 2014), the societies charged accommodation providers for televisions and radios in guest rooms **without regard to whether the rooms were occupied** — that is, they collected fees for empty rooms in which no music could have been used. The authority found this an unfair commercial condition, in breach of both Czech competition law and Article 102 TFEU. [39][40] A telling detail: OSA *had* factored room occupancy into its calculations before 18 May 2008, and stopped — a change that coincided with the introduction of a statutory cap on broadcast royalties. [41]

**The jurisdictional reversal.** The story did not end with the fines. The **Regional Court in Brno** annulled both decisions as *nicotné* (legally void), reasoning that the Ministry of Culture, not ÚOHS, is the competent regulator for the setting of royalty tariffs. [42] But in November 2023 the **Supreme Administrative Court** (Nejvyšší správní soud) reversed that view, in rulings **9 As 204/2023-59** and **6 As 210/2023-52**, holding that although collective managers occupy a specific position, *"také se jako soutěžitelé účastní hospodářské soutěže"* — they too participate in economic competition as competitors — and therefore fall squarely under the antitrust authority's oversight. [43] The fines are back in play, and the cases return to the Regional Court for reconsideration on their merits. OSA maintains that it acted lawfully throughout, in accordance with the then-applicable Copyright Act and the standard practice of foreign societies, and expects ultimately to prevail. [43]

That unresolved litigation is, in a sense, the honest ending of the whole story. The central question OSA's critics ask — *is the money being collected fairly?* — is, right now, being asked in the highest administrative courts of the country, and has not been finally answered.

## 8. Conclusion

The reflexive hostility captured by "OSA Nostra" is older than the disputes that now sustain it, and it does the debate a disservice. Collective management is not a racket; it is a genuine and difficult solution to a genuine and difficult problem — how to pay creators for uses too numerous and too small for any of them to track alone. The 88 crowns of every hundred that reach authors, the monthly payouts, the 5.2 million foreign rightsholders reachable through one Prague office: these are real services, and they are why an author registers.

And yet the criticisms are not fantasies either. A statutory monopoly that charged for empty hotel rooms until a competition authority stopped it; a "relief" law that relieves almost no one; an average royalty that flatters a distribution most of whose members barely feel it; a franchise controlled by a fraction of those it represents — these are documented, and they are the price of asking a single, unavoidable institution to hold the whole system together.

The person who plays a radio in their shop, the family that inherits a catalogue of songs, and the author who writes them are not three enemies. They are three points on a single circuit through which the same money moves — and the fairest thing that can be said about OSA is that it is where that circuit is most visible, and therefore where the argument is loudest.
